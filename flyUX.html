FlyUX Case Study

Project Summary

Searching and booking flights can be overwhelming!
Navigating clunky apps and websites is confusing and frustrating. The main objective of this project was to design an intuitive product that focused on the needs of it's users and made their journey more efficient.

My Role - UX/UI Researcher and Designer

Tools Used (Figma, SurveyMonkey, Miro)

Duration - 6 months

Process

1. Research

The main questions I wanted answered were as follows:

- What existing solutions are out there for users?
- What are users' goals while using flight booking sites?
- What do users like and where do their frustrations lie with the sites they use?

Methods used - Competitive Benchmarking, Surveys, Usability Testing

1.1 Competitive Benchmarking

Comparing the design solutions of some current industry leaders allowed me to see what already exists out there, to identify a few obvious conventions and also some painpoints all of which I took on board for my own design later.

I compared the following three airlines and one aggregator, focusing on three main features Homepage, Search, Select.




My Findings - Search and Select

I did a deep dive of the Search ans Select steps of each airline - it's visual design and functionality and marked those features that I found to be positive in green and those to be negative in red. I found a lot of interesting stuff here, I really enjoyed some of the features and began to put together an idea of what I wanted my own site to look and feel like.




Summarised Findings - Search and Select

Mostly same chronological order is followed throughout the processes. Sites that allow user to select all their requirements from the homepage are smoother.

Users like it when they are given options - freedom, control, flexibility of process .

The pages that utilized more whitespace are easier to read and feel less cluttered.

Clever use of colour draws the user's attention to CTA's and other important information on the page.

Emojis/symbols are cleverly used as indicators - reassurance.

Date pickers that provide prices are very useful.

Useful error messages error and a recovery process are essential.


1.2 Survey

After digging into the different booking processes myself, I turned to the users to learn about their experiences and habits around flight booking.

I collected 15 survey responses, asked mostly closed-ended questions but also wanted to give the option to write a short phrase to find out some emotions.


My Findings
🔗 Full Survey here here

Carrying out the survey helped to gather very valuable data, both quantitative and qualitative. I started to get an understanding of user behaviours, perceptions and frustrations. This exercise also allowed me build on what I found while benchmarking by relating some of information from the survey to my existing findings.


Summarised Findings - Survey

Most people fly at least 3 times a year and mostly prefer to book their flights on a desktop.

Price is the most important factor for most people when finding the right flight.

Price comparison sites are used to check prices and cheapest is booked through an aggregator or directly on the airlines site, users usually coming back to the aggregators several times before purchasing tickets. Most users monitor the flights for about 2 weeks before booking.

More than half of users have reported having a good flight booking experience and a large portion of users have no opinion they do what needs to be done and move on.

The main frustrations are: lack of clarity of process + lack of details about trip along the way eg. total price/add-ons, too much irrelevant information eg.promotions/upselling, need for easier access to alternative routes, more customization per user - who they travel with, where they travel from / to most frequently.

"Easy, Smooth, Efficient, Convenient, Practical, Stressful, Tedious, Meh".

1.3 Usability Testing

Conducting usability testing helped me to get a deep insight into user behaviour while they flow through the process of booking a flight in real time. It was an excellent exercise to observe what actions they took, to understand why they took them and how they felt about their user journey.

My Findings

I carried out a usability test on the Aer Lingus flight booking site and watched recorded usability tests of two other participants using the same site to gather more information.
This exercise was also a chance for me to compare my own experiences and perceptions (so far) of the flight booking process with those of different users. It put into perspective the power of mental mapping as well as the differences in digital literacy.



Findings - Usability Testing

The biggest struggle for users in all usability tests was the lack of clear indicators and subtlety of CTA's.

Most important information needs to be made very obvious, the user must not have to look for it..

Users did not like extra clicking eg. to view what was included in their fare.

One user pointed out hat they were used to adding items to their shopping cart on Amazon and would like to see the same function on flight booking sites.

Fare information should be transparent and clear.

Too much information at any one stage confused users. Information should be displayed in smaller blocks, so the user can digest it better.

Displaying alternative options was positively met by users eg. alternative dates and prices.

There is definitely a feeling of mistrust towards airlines. Most users were looking out for hidden charges throughout the process.

Banner ads, pop-ups distract from the process, cause frustration and make the site feel cheaper.

2. Analysis

After collecting the data it was time to review and make sense of it, to categorize the findings and to make a more structured overview of the user problem. This was done by creating an Affinity Diagram and Customer Journey Map.


2.1 Affinity Diagram

For this part, I worked together with another designer who was working on the same project. Together, we created a very detailed affinity map by sharing research data with each other and noting our observations on post-its. After combining all of them together, we grouped them according to different patterns that we began to see emerging. Each sub group received a name.

We came up with 25 groups in total, some of the main groups were - mental models that hinder good user experience (friction), mental models that aid good user experience, pre-booking behaviours, error recovery, communication between passengers.

You can see more of the affinity map groups here .






2.2 Customer Journey Map (CJM)

I took the grouped information from the affinity map and arranged it in line with each step of the user journey. At each step I noted the goals, behaviours, pain points and positive reactions/feedback.
The map allowed me to get a more cohesive view of where friction occurred along the process, what had caused it and gave good insight into what users enjoyed about their experience.
The top part of the CJM includes a mood map to visualize user's feelings towards the process at each step.

Summarised Findings - CJM

Users often found themselves unsure of how to proceed to the next step, which indicates a lack of feedback and indicators poorly designed CTA's.

Although users like to be given alternative options/actions, too many options and too much/unclear information is overwhelming and takes away from the user experience.

Everyone was used to different ways (mental models at work) of inputting information - typing/selecting and when their preferred method was unavailable, they were upset. It is important to incorporate that into designing the interactions.

Users found the presence of a Chatbot for quick customer support reassuring.

3. Design

Once I analysed my research and made some conclusions, it was time to begin designing my own solution.
I wanted to create a communicative product with a focus on feedback from the system to the user in the form of useful error messages and actionable recovery tasks, clear CTA's and indicators. I wanted the software to be friendly and polite, easy to navigate and feel smooth.

The steps involved in the design stage were creating a clear User Flow and some Low Fidelity Sketching.

Keeping in mind the pain-points and mental models that I took notes on, I put together a high level user flow diagram which represents how the user would interact with the system.





3.2 Low Fidelity Sketching

The next step was to start combining the user interactions with some visuals. At this stage a lot of ideas were formed and using pen and paper was a great way to experiment and make quick changes.

Navigation: Primary navigation on top right, visible at all times for easy access, secondary navigation would disappear as flight search results are displayed to keep them focused on the task and eliminate distractions

Linear Flow: Flow here is linear and I wanted the user to have the flexibility to change their flight requirements eg. luggage, number of passengers, flight class, at any time during the journey provided it's before final flight selection

Progress Bar: Feedback on position along the process

Search Forms: Easy to complete by typing or scrolling to select departure/destination city, suggesting airports nearby and last searches

Date Picker: Constraints > guide user better, eg. greying out past dates after selecting departure date

CTA's and indicators: Attention on key info, easy to identify CTA's

I used Figma to design this mid fidelity prototype and the Beta version of interactive components to create and reuse some of the more complex interactions. I then used this prototype in the validation stage and it can be used to build upon over time and improved.

5. Validation

I was able to gather evidence and feedback about the user journey that I created by testing my prototype. This feedback can be used to improve/re-design different features of the site.
It was important to let the user know that this is a simulated site and that it has limited functionality. As well as asking them to complete the booking, I also asked some questions regarding the visuals and the emotions associated with it.


I was pretty happy with how this exercise went. The user went through the process with ease and they felt that they received good feedback/confirmation from the system. Their feedback about navigation and progress bar was positive and CTA's appeared to be clear. The user did not question where they needed to go next and did not express a need for more detailed info, which was one of the things I was very curious to test.

This exercise proves very useful and from the very first step I could already see a small area for improvement, I also managed to take some notes throughout the process and take the user's ideas on board. If I were to add some re-designs, I would add prices to the date picker to make cost fluctuations more evident. I would also add a "Sign-In" option at the "Payment" step.

Conclusions

What I learned - I learned a number of things throughout this project, the main ones being how to conduct a usability test and the types of different research methods used in UX. I found the usability test particularly valuable to the project, it gave me a lot of information to work with and a confidence that I was solving a real problem.
The other very important thing I learned is that the user's mental maps will dictate their behaviour and that we need to try and understand those mental maps as best as possible.

What I would do differently - I would change up my research strategy. I would try to collect a larger sample size for my surveys. I would also perhaps try to facilitate a focus group with some better-qualified interviewees. I would like to have asked more specific questions regarding some of the features currently used on flight booking sites/apps as well as user habits.
