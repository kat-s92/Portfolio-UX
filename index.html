FlyUX Case Study

Project Summary

Searching and booking flights can be overwhelming!
Navigating clunky apps and websites is confusing and frustrating. The main objective of this project was to design an intuitive product that focused on the needs of it's users and made their journey more efficient.

My Role - UX/UI Researcher and Designer

Tools Used

Duration - 6 months

Process

1. Research

First things first - in order to solve a problem, I needed to identify it. The first step of this project was to conduct extensive research into the flight booking space and it's users.

The main questions I wanted answered were as follows:

- What existing solutions are out there for users?
- What are users' goals while using flight booking sites?
- What do users like and where do their frustrations lie with the sites they use?

Methods I used to find the answers - Competitive Benchmarking, Surveys, Usability Testing

1.1 Competitive Benchmarking

Comparing the design solutions of some current industry leaders allowed me to see what already exists out there, to identify a few obvious conventions and also some painpoints all of which I took on board for my own design later.

I compared the following three airlines and one aggregator, focusing on three main features Homepage, Search, Select.




My Findings - Homepage
üîó Search and Select Analysis here

I did a deep dive of each homepage - it's visual design and functionality and marked those features that I found to be positive in green and those to be negative in red. I found that all homepages followed some conventions and had many things in common.



Summarised Findings - Homepage

All four homepages allow the users to access all of the product's functionality without having to scroll, this is done through clear primary and secondary navigation.
Moving images, ads distracted from the task at hand. Too many features also caused confusion.
The homepages that utilized more whitespace are easier to read and feel less cluttered.
Clever use of colour draws the user's attention to CTA's and other important information on the page.
Some limitation to languages can be observed, which makes the design less inclusive.
Chatbots follow the user as they scroll down the page.


1.2 Survey

Carrying out the survey helped to gather some quantitative data and get a better understanding of user behaviours, perceptions and frustrations that they have while booking flights online.


My Findings
üîó Full Survey here here

I had 15 people take my survey. I wanted to try and get an understanding for how users feel about the flight booking experience and the websites/apps that they currently use. I asked mostly closed-ended questions but also wanted to give the option to write a short phrase to find out some emotions.


Summarised Findings - Survey

Most people fly at least 3 times a year and mostly prefer to book their flights on a desktop.
Price is the most important factor for most people when finding the right flight.
Price comparison sites are used to check prices and cheapest is booked through an aggregator or directly on the airlines site.
Almost nobody books their flights on the spot, they come back to check and browse/monitor prices/offers.
More than half of users have a good flight booking experience and a large portion of users have no opinion they do what needs to be done and move on.
The main frustrations are: lack of clarity of process + lack of details about trip along the way eg. total price/add-ons, too much irrelevant information eg.promotions/upselling, need for easier access to alternative routes, more customization per user - who they travel with, where they travel from / to most frequently.
Some of the Emotions - "Easy, Smooth, Efficient, Convenient, Practical, Stressful, Tedious, Meh".

1.3 Usability Testing

Conducting usability testing helped me to get a deep insight into user behaviour while they flow through the process of booking a flight in real time. It was an excellent exercise to see what actions they took, to understand why they took them and how they felt about their user journey.

My Findings

I carried out usability tests on two different flight booking sites - Aer Lingus and Eurowings. As well as that I watched usability tests of two other participants to gather more information.



Summarised Findings - Usability Testing

The biggest struggle for users in all usability tests was the lack of clear indicators and subtlety of some CTA's.
Most important information needs to be made very obvious, the user must not have to look for it.
Users did not like extra clicking eg. to view what was included in their fare.
One user pointed out hat they were used to adding items to their shopping cart on Amazon and would like to see the same function on flight booking sites.
Fare information should be transparent and clear.
Too much information at any one stage confused users.
Displaying alternative options was positively met by users eg. alternative dates and prices.
There is definitely a feeling of mistrust towards airlines.
Banner ads, pop-ups distract from the process and cause frustration.

2. Analysis

After collecting the data it was time to review and make sense of it, to categorize the findings and to make a more structured overview of the user problem. This was done by creating an Affinity Diagram and Customer Journey Map.


2.1 Affinity Diagram

I worked together with another designer on an a very detailed affinity map. We shared our research data with each other and noted our observations on post-its. After combining all of them together, we grouped them together according to different patterns that we began to see emerging. Each sub group received a name.

We came up with 25 groups in total, some of the main groups were - mental models that hinder good user experience (friction), mental models that aid good user experience, pre-booking behaviours, error recovery, communication between passengers.

You can see more of the affinity map groups here .






2.2 Customer Journey Map (CJM)

I took the grouped information from the affinity map and arranged it in line with each step of the user journey. At each step I noted the goals, behaviours, pain points and positive reactions/feedback.
The map allowed me to get a more cohesive view of where friction occurred along the process, what had caused it and gave good insight into what users enjoyed about their experience.
The top part of the CJM includes a mood map to visualize user's feelings towards the process at each step.

Summarised Findings - CJM

Users often found themselves unsure of how to proceed to the next step, which indicates a lack of feedback and indicators poorly designed CTA's
Too many options and too much/unclear information is overwhelming and takes away from the user experience
Everyone was used to different ways (mental models at work) of inputting information - typing/selecting and when their preferred method was unavailable, they were upset
Users preferred a clean design with lots of whitespace and some bright colours
Users found the presence of a Chatbot for quick customer support reassuring

3. Design

Once I was done with the research analysis, it was time to begin designing my own solution.
I wanted to create an intuitive and communicative product with a focus on good user feedback from the system - useful error messages and actionable recovery tasks, clear CTA's and indicators and an overall friendly, polite software.

The steps involved in the design stage were creating a clear User Flow , sketching the Interaction Design and some Low Fidelity Wireframing.

3.1 User Flow Diagram
üîó More detailed version here

Keeping in mind the pain-points and mental models that I took notes on, I put together a high level user flow diagram which represents how the user would interact with the system.





3.2 Interaction Design
üîó Sketches

The next step was to start putting my ideas on paper - I sketched what the screens would look like and how the user will interact with each feature on the page.
Building on everything that I had learned up to that point and keeping to the current conventions I had a good idea of what I wanted the user experience to feel

Navigation: Primary navigation on top right, visible at all times for easy access, secondary navigation would disappear as flight search results are displayed to keep them focused on the task and eliminate distractions

Linear Flow: Flow here is linear and I wanted the user to have the flexibility to change their flight requirements eg. luggage, number of passengers, flight class, at any time during the journey provided it's before final flight selection

Progress Bar: Giving users feedback on how far along in the process they are
Search Forms: Easy to complete by typing or scrolling to select departure/destination city, suggesting airports nearby and last searches
Date Picker: Would have some constraints in order to guide the user better, eg. greying out all past dates after selecting departure date
