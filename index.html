FlyUX Case Study

Project Summary

Searching and booking flights can be overwhelming!
Navigating clunky apps and websites is confusing and frustrating. The main objective of this project was to design an intuitive product that focused on the needs of it's users and made their journey more efficient.

My Role - UX/UI Researcher and Designer

Tools Used

Duration - 6 months

Process

1. Research

First thing is first - in order to solve a problem, I needed to identify it. The first step of this project was to conduct extensive research into the flight booking space and it's users.

The main questions I wanted answered were as follows:

- What existing solutions are out there for users?
- What are users' goals while using flight booking sites?
- What do users like and where do their frustrations lie with the sites they use?

Methods I used to find the answers - Competitive Benchmarking, Surveys, Usability Testing

1.1 Competitive Benchmarking

Comparing the design solutions of some current industry leaders allowed me to see what already exists out there, to identify a few obvious conventions and also some painpoints all of which I took onbaord for my own design later.

I compared the following three airlines and one aggregator, focusing on three main features Homepage, Search, Select.




My Findings - Homepage

I did a deep dive of each homepage - it's visual design and functionality and marked those features that I found to be positive in green and those to be negative in red. I found that all homepages followed some convensions and had many things in common.



Summarised Findings - Homepage

All four homepages allow the users to access all of the product's functionality without having to scroll, this is done through clear primary and secondary navigation.
Moving images, ads distracted from the task at hand. Too many features also caused confusion.
The homepages that utilised more whitescapce are easier to read and feel less cluttered.
Clever use of colour draws the user's attention to CTA's and other important information on the page.
Some limitation to languages can be observed, which makes the design less inclusive.
Chatbots follow the user as they scroll down the page.

1.2 Survey

Carrying out the survey helped to gather some quantitative data and get a better understanding of user behaviours, perceptions and furstrations that they have while booking flights online.


My Findings
üîó Full Survey here here

I had 15 people take my survey. I wanted to try and get an understanding for how users feel about the flight booking experience and the websites/apps that they currently use. I asked mostly closed-ended questions but also wanted to give the option to write a short phrase to find out some emotions.


Summarised Findings - Survey

Most people fly at least 3 times a year and mostly prefer to book their flights on a desktop.
Price is the most important factor for most people when finding the right flight.
Price comparison sites are used to check prices and cheapest is booked through an aggregator or directly on the airlines site.
Almost nobody books their flights on the spot, they come back to check and browse/monitor prices/offers.
More than half of users have a good flight booking experience and a large portion of users have no opinion they do what needs to be done and move on.
The main frustrations are: lack of clarity of process + lack of details about trip along the way eg. total price/add-ons, too much irrelevant information eg.promotions/upselling, need for easier access to alternative routes, more customization per user - who they travel with, where they travel from / to most frequently.
Some of the Emotions - "Easy, Smooth, Efficient, Convenient, Practical, Stressful, Tedious, Meh".

1.3 Usability Testing

Conducting usability testing helped me to get a deep insight into user behaviour while they flow through the process of booking a flight in real time. It was an excellent exercise to see what actions they took, to understand why they took them and how they felt about their user journey.

My Findings

I carried out usability tests on two different flight booking sites - Aar Lingus and Eurowings. As well as that I watched usability tests of two other participants to gather more information.

Summarised Findings - Usability Testing

The biggest struggle for users in all usability tests was the lack of clear indicators and subtilty of some CTA's.
Most important information needs to be made very obvious, the user must not have to look for it.
Users did not like extra clicking eg. to view what was included in their fare.
One user pointed out hat they were used to adding items to their shopping cart on Amazon and would like to see the same function on flight booking sites.
Fare information should be transparent and clear.
Too much information at any one stage confused users.
Displaying alternative options was positively met by users eg. alternative dates and prices.
There is definitely a feeling of mistrust towards airlines.
Banner ads, pop-ups distract from the process and cause frustration.

2. Analysis

After collecting the data it was time to review and make sense of it, to categorise the findings and to make a more structured overview of the user problem. This was done by creating an Affinity Diagram and Customer Journey Map.


2.1 Affinity Diagram

I worked together with another designer on an a very detailed affinity map. We shared our research data with each other and noted our observations on post-its. After combining all of them togther, we grouped them togther according to different patterns that we began to see emerging. Each sub group received a name.

We came up with 25 groups in total, some of the main groups were - mental models that hinder good user experience (friction), mental models that aid good user experience, pre-booking behaviours, error recovery, communcation between passengers.
